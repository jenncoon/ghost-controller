Station 01, Decision 1

What concept are you testing?

1
One objective. Not the whole unit.
Pick the concept your students consistently miss, the thing that shows up wrong on every exam, the thing you find yourself explaining over and over. That's your game.
Design Principle, Focus Decision 1

The most common mistake in escape room design is trying to review everything. A game that covers ten objectives covers none of them well. The vault-code mechanism works because it forces a single moment of reckoning, students either understand the concept or they don't. Focus is what makes the friction real.

Station 02, Decision 2

What does success look like in the room?

2
Name the win condition before you write a single question.
One sentence. What does a student do or know at the end of your game that they couldn't do or know at the start? Make it specific and observable.
Design Principle, Backward Design Decision 2

The vault-code system works because the code is the proof. Students can't guess their way to it, they have to understand the concept to produce the right answer. Your win condition is the code. Design backward from it.

Think in three vaults. Vault 1 gets them into the story. Vault 2 makes them work. Vault 3 is the moment of discovery, the thing that surprises them about what they thought they knew.

Design in-person first. The online version follows naturally.
A sketch of how this works physically, with real students in your real classroom, is the most important input you can give an AI platform when you are ready to build the online version.
Design Principle, In-Person First Decision 3

The physical version tells you what the experience actually needs: the sequence, the friction points, the moments where students talk to each other. Once you know what must happen, the digital container is a translation problem, not a design problem.
